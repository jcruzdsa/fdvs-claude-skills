# Fan Enterprise Value Playbooks

## Working framing

Fan Enterprise Value is no longer just a model, score, or analytics asset. It is a decision platform for deciding what the NBA should do when value, risk, potential, or strategic opportunity changes.

The two playbooks serve different audiences and must stay separate.

**Playbook 1: Strategic Decision Playbook** answers: **What should the business do?**

**Playbook 2: Technical Engine Playbook** answers: **What engine does this run on?**

The strategic playbook owns the business logic. The technical playbook exists to make that logic reliable, governed, measurable, and scalable.

Core operating rule:

**No signal without an owner. No owner without an action. No action without measurement. No technical build without a decision use case.**

# Playbook 1: Fan Enterprise Value Strategic Decision Playbook

## 1. Purpose

The Fan Enterprise Value Strategic Decision Playbook defines how the business should react when FEV signals cross meaningful thresholds.

This playbook is not about how the model is built. It is about what the organization should do with the model's outputs.

FEV should help the NBA answer questions like:

- Which fan relationships should we protect?
- Which fans should we grow?
- Which fans should we educate before monetizing?
- Which fans should we avoid over-monetizing?
- Which segments deserve more investment?
- Which campaigns should scale, pause, or be remeasured?
- Which products or experiences are creating friction for valuable fans?
- Which partner or market opportunities are strategically valuable even before they fully monetize?

The purpose of this playbook is to translate FEV from a score into an operating discipline.

## 2. Strategic audience

This playbook is written for business leaders and operating teams who make fan investment decisions.

Primary audiences:

- Executive leadership
- Finance and Strategy
- CRM and Lifecycle Marketing
- Paid Media and Acquisition
- Product and DTC / League Pass
- Content and Personalization
- Partnerships and Business Development
- International and Market Development
- Analytics and Experimentation leaders

Secondary audiences:

- Data Science
- Data Engineering
- Marketing Technology
- Governance / Privacy

These technical teams are important, but they are not the main audience for Playbook 1. Their job is to help make the strategic decisions operationally possible.

## 3. Strategic premise

Fan Enterprise Value should not be treated as a blunt ranking of fans from most valuable to least valuable.

FEV is a profile of relationship value. It tells the business what kind of investment a fan relationship deserves next.

The business should stop asking only:

**What is this fan worth?**

The better question is:

**What should we do next for this fan, segment, product, market, campaign, or partner audience given its value, risk, potential, context, and confidence?**

## 4. Decision principles

### Principle 1: FEV exists to change decisions

If a signal does not change a treatment, budget, offer, roadmap priority, campaign rule, partner strategy, or market plan, it is not yet a product. It is analysis.

### Principle 2: Current revenue is not total value

A fan may be valuable through direct revenue, future growth, engagement durability, partner value, media value, content value, strategic market relevance, or long-term fandom development.

### Principle 3: Not every high-value fan should receive an offer

Some fans need retention support. Some need education. Some need better content. Some need a product fix. Some need patience. A discount is only one possible treatment and should not be the default.

### Principle 4: Strategic value requires patience

Youth fans, international fans, player-led fans, early-stage NBA ID users, and partner-led fans may have low current monetization but high long-term enterprise value.

### Principle 5: Incremental lift matters more than activity

Sends, impressions, clicks, opens, conversions, and gross revenue are not enough. Major FEV-driven decisions should be measured by incremental retained value, incremental conversion, durable engagement, avoided waste, or strategic market progress.

### Principle 6: Confidence matters

A high score with low confidence should trigger caution, measurement, or monitoring. A high score with high confidence can trigger more decisive action.

### Principle 7: Governance is part of the product

FEV must respect consent, privacy rules, approved use cases, explainability, fairness, and human decision rights.

## 5. Signal taxonomy

The business needs a shared vocabulary for the signals it will act on.

### 5.1 Fan Enterprise Value tier

Represents the estimated importance of a fan relationship to the enterprise.

Recommended business tiers:

- **Tier 1: Enterprise Critical** — highest-value or highest-strategic-importance fan relationships.
- **Tier 2: High Value** — strong direct or expected enterprise value.
- **Tier 3: Growth Value** — moderate current value with meaningful potential.
- **Tier 4: Emerging / Strategic** — low current value but strategic context or future upside.
- **Tier 5: Low Current Priority** — low current value, low observed potential, or insufficient signal.

Important: these tiers should not be used to determine fan worth as people. They are business investment categories.

### 5.2 Retention or dormancy risk

Signals whether a fan relationship appears at risk of decline, churn, inactivity, or reduced monetization.

Business interpretation:

- High risk among high-FEV fans should trigger protection.
- High risk among strategic-potential fans should trigger education or habit support.
- High risk among low-confidence fans should trigger monitoring before heavy investment.

### 5.3 Growth potential

Signals whether a fan has meaningful room to deepen relationship value.

Growth potential may come from:

- High engagement but low monetization
- Strong favorite team or player affinity
- Repeated app or content behavior
- NBA ID activity
- Fantasy, badge, challenge, or program activity
- Product propensity
- Market growth context
- Partner-permitted signals

### 5.4 Opportunity gap

The difference between what a fan appears likely to value and what they are currently doing.

Examples:

- Watches highlights but has no League Pass trial or subscription
- Follows a favorite team but has no schedule alerts
- Engages with merch content but has no commerce purchase
- Opens NBA emails but does not use app features
- Has strong player affinity but receives generic messages
- Is high value but recently failed onboarding or renewal

### 5.5 Strategic value

Signals that the fan, segment, market, or audience is important for enterprise strategy even if current direct revenue is low.

Examples:

- International market development
- Youth fandom
- WNBA adjacency
- Emerging basketball markets
- Player-led demand
- Partner-led reach
- Future DTC potential
- High media or sponsor relevance

### 5.6 Confidence level

Signals how much trust the business should place in the score.

Recommended levels:

- **High confidence** — sufficient history, stable signal, explainable drivers.
- **Medium confidence** — useful signal but some uncertainty.
- **Low confidence** — sparse data, conflicting signals, stale inputs, or unstable behavior.

Confidence should change the action. Low confidence does not mean ignore; it often means test, monitor, or collect more signal.

## 6. Action taxonomy

FEV should route the business toward a limited set of action types.

### Protect

Used when a valuable fan relationship is at risk.

Examples:

- Retention journey
- Save path
- Support intervention
- Product education
- Friction diagnosis
- Targeted benefit or offer if justified

### Grow

Used when a fan has meaningful upside and is ready for deeper engagement or monetization.

Examples:

- Upgrade journey
- Cross-sell
- Premium feature education
- Product bundle
- Commerce or ticketing path

### Educate

Used when the fan has potential but needs orientation, habit formation, or contextual relevance before monetization.

Examples:

- Explain how to follow a team
- Explain League Pass value
- Introduce favorite player or team content
- Promote app features
- Onboard into alerts, watchlist, or NBA ID benefits

### Personalize

Used when a known fan context should change content, message, product surface, or offer.

Examples:

- Team-based content
- Player-based journeys
- International/localized content
- Schedule-based reminders
- Viewing preference-based recommendations

### Suppress

Used when spend or contact is unlikely to create enterprise value.

Examples:

- Reduce paid media bids
- Suppress from high-cost retargeting
- Limit promotional pressure
- Avoid unnecessary lifecycle sends

### Test

Used when value appears possible but incrementality is unknown.

Examples:

- Holdout
- A/B test
- Matched control
- Geo test
- Incrementality read

### Invest

Used for segments, products, programs, or markets where long-term upside justifies resources.

Examples:

- Content localization
- Product packaging
- Market development
- Partner development
- Roadmap prioritization

### Monitor

Used when signal is promising but confidence is not strong enough for direct intervention.

Examples:

- Track movement
- Collect more signal
- Add to watchlist
- Reassess after next event window

### Escalate

Used when a signal has cross-functional or executive implications.

Examples:

- High-value segment deteriorating
- Strategic market accelerating
- Campaign creating value leakage
- Product issue affecting high-FEV fans
- Partner audience showing unexpected value or cannibalization

## 7. Strategic decision plays

Each play below defines a business trigger, interpretation, owner, action, guardrail, and measurement standard.

## Play 1: Protect high-value fans at risk

### Trigger

Fan is Tier 1 or Tier 2 FEV and has high retention, churn, dormancy, or renewal risk.

### Business interpretation

This is retained enterprise value at risk. The business should treat the fan relationship as worth protecting before the decline becomes harder or more expensive to reverse.

### Primary owner

CRM / Lifecycle Marketing

### Supporting owners

Product, DTC, Support, Analytics, Data Science

### Recommended actions

- Route into a save-path journey.
- Use reason codes to diagnose likely risk driver.
- If risk is content-related, personalize around team, player, schedule, or game moments.
- If risk is product-related, route toward education, support, or friction resolution.
- If risk is price or renewal-related, evaluate offer eligibility.
- Use offers selectively, not as the default.

### Guardrails

- Do not discount before diagnosing risk.
- Do not over-message high-value fans without consent and contact governance.
- Do not treat all churn risk as price sensitivity.

### Measurement

- Incremental retention lift
- Retained enterprise value
- Renewal lift
- Engagement recovery
- Support resolution impact
- Offer cost versus retained value

## Play 2: Grow high-potential, low-revenue fans

### Trigger

Fan has high growth potential, strong engagement, and low or zero current direct revenue.

### Business interpretation

This fan may be under-monetized, under-educated, or not yet connected to the right product. The goal is to develop value, not immediately extract value.

### Primary owner

Product Growth / Lifecycle Marketing

### Supporting owners

DTC, Content, CRM, Analytics

### Recommended actions

- Send product education rather than immediate discount.
- Route to team, player, schedule, or feature-based personalization.
- Introduce League Pass, app features, NBA ID benefits, alerts, watchlists, or fantasy-related experiences.
- Test conversion journeys by audience type.
- Use first-purchase, trial, or upgrade paths when product fit is clear.

### Guardrails

- Do not assume zero revenue means low value.
- Do not push paid conversion before establishing relevance.
- Do not judge success only by immediate conversion.

### Measurement

- First paid conversion
- Trial start and trial-to-paid conversion
- Durable engagement after treatment
- Incremental revenue
- Incremental enterprise value
- Value per treated fan

## Play 3: Use strategic patience for high-potential, low-current-value fans

### Trigger

Fan, segment, or market has low current direct revenue but high strategic value.

Examples include international fans, youth fans, emerging-market fans, player-led fans, or fans with strong engagement but limited current product availability.

### Business interpretation

This is a market-development or fandom-development opportunity. The right action may be education, localization, content, habit formation, or partner strategy instead of direct monetization.

### Primary owner

International / Strategy

### Supporting owners

Content, Product, Partnerships, CRM, Analytics

### Recommended actions

- Build localized or context-aware content journeys.
- Develop team/player affinity paths.
- Encourage registration, favorites, alerts, app usage, or program participation.
- Evaluate product packaging and payment barriers.
- Consider partner-led development before direct monetization.
- Track strategic progress separately from current revenue.

### Guardrails

- Do not compare early-stage international markets directly to mature domestic DTC markets.
- Do not suppress investment solely because current revenue is low.
- Do not monetize too aggressively before fandom depth develops.

### Measurement

- Registered fan growth
- Repeat engagement
- Favorite team/player capture
- App habit formation
- Localized content engagement
- Market FEV growth
- Partner opportunity creation

## Play 4: Suppress inefficient spend against low-value, low-potential audiences

### Trigger

Fan or audience has low FEV, low growth potential, low engagement quality, and low confidence of future value creation.

### Business interpretation

This audience may not justify expensive paid media, aggressive retargeting, or high-frequency CRM treatment.

### Primary owner

Paid Media / Acquisition

### Supporting owners

Finance, Analytics, CRM, Data Science

### Recommended actions

- Reduce bid pressure.
- Suppress from high-cost retargeting.
- Shift budget toward higher-value or higher-potential audiences.
- Use lower-cost nurture if contact is still appropriate.
- Reassess after major calendar moments or signal changes.

### Guardrails

- Do not permanently exclude fans from access or experience.
- Do not use suppression where strategic, fairness, or governance concerns apply.
- Do not suppress solely on current revenue when strategic value exists.

### Measurement

- Avoided inefficient spend
- CAC improvement
- Payback improvement
- Value per acquired fan
- Campaign ROI improvement

## Play 5: Upgrade high-value fans with clear product fit

### Trigger

Fan has high FEV, high product propensity, and a clear product or feature fit.

### Business interpretation

This fan may be ready for expansion into a higher-value product, package, feature, bundle, or experience.

### Primary owner

Product Growth / DTC

### Supporting owners

CRM, Content, Commerce, Ticketing, Analytics

### Recommended actions

- Route to upgrade journey.
- Promote premium features or package benefits.
- Personalize based on team, player, viewing behavior, device behavior, or calendar moment.
- Consider bundles, commerce, tickets, or partner experiences where relevant.
- Use product education before promotional incentives.

### Guardrails

- Do not over-promote irrelevant products.
- Do not confuse propensity with incremental value.
- Do not create a worse fan experience by pushing monetization too frequently.

### Measurement

- Upgrade conversion
- Expansion revenue
- Incremental enterprise value
- Repeat engagement after upgrade
- Churn impact
- Offer cost versus value generated

## Play 6: Fix product friction for valuable drop-offs

### Trigger

High-FEV or high-potential fans show onboarding failure, failed activation, viewing drop-off, payment friction, support friction, device friction, blackout confusion, or repeated abandonment.

### Business interpretation

The business may be leaking value because the product experience is blocking valuable fans from succeeding.

### Primary owner

Product

### Supporting owners

DTC, Support, Analytics, Engineering, CRM

### Recommended actions

- Identify friction cluster and affected high-value audience.
- Prioritize roadmap fixes based on value at risk.
- Trigger support, education, or recovery journeys.
- Build targeted product nudges where appropriate.
- Escalate severe value leakage to product leadership.

### Guardrails

- Do not treat product friction only as a marketing problem.
- Do not continue acquisition spend into broken journeys without remediation.
- Do not rely only on aggregate product metrics; inspect high-value drop-offs.

### Measurement

- Recovered activation
- Reduced abandonment
- Support resolution
- Retained value
- Product usage recovery
- Downstream conversion or renewal lift

## Play 7: Re-rank segments by enterprise value, not just size

### Trigger

A segment shows high total FEV, high average FEV, fast value growth, high risk concentration, or a meaningful opportunity gap.

### Business interpretation

The segment may deserve more attention than its raw size or current revenue suggests.

### Primary owner

Strategy / Finance

### Supporting owners

CRM, Product, Content, International, Analytics

### Recommended actions

- Compare segment size, average value, total value, risk, and growth rate.
- Prioritize segments for campaigns, content, product improvements, or market strategy.
- Identify whether the right reaction is protect, grow, educate, invest, or monitor.
- Reallocate budget or attention where justified.

### Guardrails

- Do not prioritize only the largest segments.
- Do not prioritize only the highest-spending segments.
- Do not ignore confidence, sample size, or measurement quality.

### Measurement

- Segment value growth
- Incremental lift by segment
- Retained value
- Conversion quality
- Budget reallocation impact

## Play 8: Require incrementality before scaling campaigns

### Trigger

Campaign shows strong gross conversions, revenue, or engagement, but incremental enterprise value is unclear.

### Business interpretation

The campaign may be taking credit for behavior that would have happened anyway, or it may be generating low-quality value.

### Primary owner

Marketing Analytics / Experimentation

### Supporting owners

Campaign Owner, CRM, Paid Media, Finance

### Recommended actions

- Add holdout, matched control, A/B test, geo test, or incrementality design.
- Separate gross revenue from incremental enterprise value.
- Evaluate value per treated fan and cost per incremental value.
- Scale only when impact is defensible.

### Guardrails

- Do not scale only because conversions look high.
- Do not compare campaigns without accounting for audience quality.
- Do not claim enterprise value without measurement discipline.

### Measurement

- Incremental enterprise value
- Incremental conversion lift
- Payback period
- Cost per incremental value
- Confidence band

## Play 9: Separate partner influence from owned-channel value

### Trigger

Partner-linked fans or partner-engaged audiences show high value, but overlap, cannibalization, or incrementality is unclear.

### Business interpretation

The partner may be creating value, reaching valuable fans, or simply overlapping with fans who were already valuable. The business needs contribution logic before using FEV in partner claims or packages.

### Primary owner

Partnerships

### Supporting owners

Analytics, Data Governance, Finance, Legal / Privacy, CRM

### Recommended actions

- Define approved partner value use case.
- Identify overlap with owned-channel audiences.
- Estimate incremental partner contribution.
- Build partner fan quality index.
- Use findings to shape packages, renewals, and partner strategy.

### Guardrails

- Do not double count partner and owned-channel value.
- Do not expose or activate data outside approved usage.
- Do not claim partner lift without a measurement method.

### Measurement

- Incremental partner value
- Fan quality index
- Repeat behavior
- Downstream conversion impact
- Cannibalization estimate
- Partner package performance

## Play 10: Escalate market-development opportunities

### Trigger

A market, region, or international audience shows rising engagement, high strategic value, strong player/team affinity, or under-monetized demand.

### Business interpretation

This may require coordinated strategy across content, product, partnerships, packaging, and market development.

### Primary owner

International / Market Strategy

### Supporting owners

Content, Product, Partnerships, Finance, Analytics

### Recommended actions

- Build market opportunity brief.
- Assess local context, language, time zone, payment, players, teams, clubs, and partner channels.
- Identify whether the market needs content investment, product packaging, partnership development, or event/community strategy.
- Track current value separately from future strategic value.

### Guardrails

- Do not judge emerging markets only by current DTC revenue.
- Do not assume U.S. fan behavior patterns transfer cleanly.
- Do not launch generic campaigns where local context matters.

### Measurement

- Market FEV growth
- Registered fan growth
- Repeat engagement
- Localized content performance
- Product readiness
- Partner pipeline
- Strategic milestone progress

## 8. Threshold design

This playbook should not begin with arbitrary fixed thresholds. Thresholds should be designed around decision economics.

Recommended threshold approach:

### 8.1 Use relative tiers first

For early operating use, start with percentile-based tiers:

- Top 1%: Enterprise Critical
- Top 2-10%: High Value
- Top 10-30%: Growth Value
- Middle 30-70%: Standard / Monitor
- Bottom 30%: Low Current Priority or Sparse Signal

These should be calibrated by business context, not blindly applied everywhere.

### 8.2 Pair value with action cost

A high-cost action should require higher value, higher confidence, or stronger expected lift.

Examples:

- Low-cost personalization can apply broadly.
- Medium-cost CRM journeys require clearer value or potential.
- High-cost offers require value, risk, eligibility, and expected incremental retention.
- Product roadmap escalation requires value concentration and repeated friction evidence.

### 8.3 Use confidence gates

Recommended logic:

- High value + high confidence: act.
- High value + medium confidence: test or act with guardrails.
- High value + low confidence: monitor, collect signal, or test lightly.
- Low value + high strategic context: do not suppress automatically.
- Low value + low potential + high confidence: reduce expensive treatment.

### 8.4 Separate fan, segment, product, campaign, partner, and market thresholds

The same threshold should not apply across all decision objects.

- Fan thresholds drive treatment.
- Segment thresholds drive planning.
- Product thresholds drive roadmap and packaging.
- Campaign thresholds drive scale/pause decisions.
- Partner thresholds drive contribution and package strategy.
- Market thresholds drive investment patience and localization.

## 9. Ownership model

FEV should not create a new bottleneck where one product owner approves every action. The playbook should define decision rights and make each function accountable.

| Area | Owns | Must partner with | Primary decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan Value Product Lead | FEV framework, playbook, score interpretation, prioritization logic | All functions | What signals mean and how they should be used |
| CRM / Lifecycle | Save, winback, onboarding, nurture, conversion journeys | Analytics, Product, Data Science | Which fans receive which lifecycle treatments |
| Product / DTC | Product fit, upgrade paths, onboarding, friction fixes | CRM, Analytics, Engineering | Which product actions or fixes are justified by value |
| Paid Media | Bidding, suppression, acquisition quality | Finance, Analytics, Data Science | Where paid spend should scale or pull back |
| Partnerships | Partner audience value, package strategy, contribution claims | Analytics, Governance, Finance | How partner-linked value should influence commercial strategy |
| International / Market Strategy | Country and regional value strategy | Content, Product, Partnerships, Finance | Where strategic patience or market investment is justified |
| Finance / Strategy | Investment standards, budget logic, business cases | Product Lead, Analytics, Business Owners | Whether value economics justify spend or scale |
| Analytics / Experimentation | Lift design, holdouts, readouts, confidence | All action owners | Whether actions created incremental value |
| Data Science | Models, calibration, reason codes, confidence, drift | Product Lead, Analytics, Data Engineering | Whether signals are reliable and explainable |
| Data Engineering / Platform | Pipelines, serving, reliability, access, lineage | Data Science, Activation Teams | Whether signals are available and trustworthy |
| Governance / Privacy | Approved use, consent, privacy, fairness controls | Legal, Data, Business Owners | Whether a use case is allowed and safe |

## 10. Operating cadence

### Weekly activation review

Audience: CRM, Product, Paid Media, Analytics, Fan Value Product Lead

Purpose:

- Review active FEV-triggered plays.
- Identify new triggered audiences.
- Confirm owners and treatments.
- Review early performance indicators.
- Escalate blockers.

Output:

- Active play list
- Owner assignments
- Experiment status
- Immediate decisions

### Monthly value review

Audience: Business owners, Finance, Analytics, Data Science, Product Lead

Purpose:

- Review FEV movement by fan tier, segment, product, campaign, and market.
- Identify value leakage and value growth.
- Compare actions against outcomes.
- Decide which plays scale, pause, or change.

Output:

- Monthly FEV performance readout
- Scale / pause / revise decisions
- Updated threshold recommendations

### Quarterly investment review

Audience: Executive leadership, Finance, Strategy, Product, International, Partnerships

Purpose:

- Use FEV to inform broader investment decisions.
- Reassess market, product, partner, and segment priorities.
- Approve larger roadmap, budget, or partnership implications.

Output:

- Investment recommendations
- Strategic segment or market priorities
- Governance or measurement decisions

## 11. Measurement standards

Every play should identify the right measurement method before launch.

### Preferred measurement methods

- Randomized holdout
- A/B test
- Matched control
- Geo test
- Pre/post with strong controls
- Difference-in-differences where appropriate
- Cohort comparison where stronger methods are not feasible

### Primary success metrics

- Incremental enterprise value
- Retained enterprise value
- Incremental conversion
- Durable engagement lift
- Product activation recovery
- Avoided media waste
- Market growth signal
- Partner contribution lift

### Metrics to use cautiously

- Gross revenue
- Opens
- Clicks
- Impressions
- Total conversions
- Total engagement
- Last-touch attribution

These may be useful diagnostics, but they should not be the final proof of FEV impact.

## 12. First 90-day operating plan

### Days 0-30: Define the decision system

- Finalize signal taxonomy.
- Finalize action taxonomy.
- Approve the first 8-10 decision plays.
- Assign owners for each play.
- Define initial threshold logic.
- Identify the first two or three pilot plays.
- Confirm measurement approach.

Recommended first pilots:

1. High-FEV / high-risk League Pass save path.
2. High-engagement / low-revenue conversion path.
3. High-value product friction recovery path.

### Days 31-60: Launch controlled pilots

- Activate pilot audiences.
- Implement treatment rules.
- Establish holdouts or comparison groups.
- Review early quality signals.
- Confirm operational handoffs.
- Document exceptions and decision gaps.

### Days 61-90: Read, refine, and scale

- Report incremental lift.
- Identify which plays scale.
- Adjust thresholds.
- Identify technical gaps exposed by activation.
- Prepare executive readout.
- Expand to additional plays only when ownership and measurement are clear.

## 13. Strategic playbook takeaway

The strategic playbook exists to make FEV operational.

The goal is not to admire value scores. The goal is to change what the NBA does:

- Protect valuable relationships earlier.
- Grow high-potential fans more intelligently.
- Stop wasting spend where value is unlikely.
- Avoid over-monetizing strategic fans too early.
- Fix product friction where value is leaking.
- Measure incrementality before scaling claims.
- Reallocate investment toward durable fandom.

# Playbook 2: Fan Enterprise Value Technical Engine Playbook

## 1. Purpose

The Fan Enterprise Value Technical Engine Playbook explains how the decision platform runs underneath the strategic playbook.

It defines the data, models, rules, serving layers, activation paths, measurement systems, and governance controls required to make FEV reliable and usable.

This playbook should not lead the strategy. It should support the strategy.

The engine exists to answer:

- What data powers FEV?
- How are value, risk, potential, and confidence signals generated?
- How are business thresholds translated into decision rules?
- How are signals delivered to the systems that act on them?
- How are actions measured?
- How are model changes, data issues, privacy rules, and score drift governed?

## 2. Technical audience

Primary audiences:

- Data Engineering
- Data Science
- ML Engineering
- Analytics Engineering
- Marketing Technology / CRM Ops
- CDP / Activation Platform owners
- Product Analytics
- Experimentation / Measurement
- Data Governance / Privacy
- BI / Reporting teams

Secondary audiences:

- CRM / Lifecycle
- Product / DTC
- Paid Media
- Partnerships
- International
- Finance / Strategy

Business teams should understand the technical engine at a conceptual level, but they should not need to inspect pipelines or model mechanics to use FEV responsibly.

## 3. Technical premise

The FEV engine is not one model. It is a decision infrastructure made of several connected layers.

Recommended architecture:

1. Source data layer
2. Feature and identity layer
3. Signal generation layer
4. Decision rules layer
5. Serving and activation layer
6. Measurement layer
7. Governance and monitoring layer

Each layer should map back to one or more strategic decision plays.

If a technical capability does not support a decision play, it should not be prioritized ahead of capabilities that do.

## 4. Source data layer

The source layer provides the raw ingredients for FEV signals.

### 4.1 Core fan identity inputs

- Fan ID
- NBA ID
- Identity graph keys
- Email / CRM IDs where permitted
- Device or platform identifiers where permitted
- Household or account relationships where appropriate and approved
- Consent and contact eligibility

### 4.2 Revenue and transaction inputs

- League Pass / DTC subscription revenue
- Subscription plan, SKU, tenure, renewal status
- Commerce / Fanatics revenue where permitted
- Ticketing revenue and attendance
- Vision or other product revenue
- Attributed acquisition revenue
- Refunds, cancellations, chargebacks where available
- Margin or contribution inputs where available

### 4.3 Engagement inputs

- App engagement
- Web engagement
- Email sends, opens, clicks
- Push engagement
- Watch activity
- Highlights, articles, video, and content consumption
- Fantasy participation
- Badges, challenges, programs
- Favorite team / player behavior
- First activity and last activity
- Seasonality and event-window behavior

### 4.4 Product and subscription inputs

- Product SKU
- Subscription length
- Payment platform
- Renewal date
- Cancellation date
- Trial status
- Device usage
- Feature usage
- Onboarding completion
- Product friction indicators

### 4.5 Campaign and marketing inputs

- Campaign ID
- Channel
- Creative
- Audience definition
- Treatment date
- Campaign cost
- Tracking code
- Attribution method
- Holdout / experiment flag

### 4.6 Partner inputs

- Partner ID
- Partner audience membership
- Partner engagement signals where permitted
- Approved usage scope
- Overlap with owned channels
- Partner revenue or influenced activity

### 4.7 Market and strategic inputs

- Country
- Region
- City
- Language where available and permitted
- Local time zone
- Favorite team / player with market relevance
- International market priority
- Event or content calendar
- Local product availability
- Payment availability or limitations

## 5. Feature and identity layer

The feature layer converts source data into reusable, governed inputs.

### 5.1 Feature spine

The fan-level feature spine should provide a stable daily view of fan attributes, behavior, value history, and eligibility.

Recommended requirements:

- One row per fan per score date
- Stable fan identifier
- Refresh timestamp
- Source lineage
- Consent flags
- Contact eligibility flags
- Feature freshness indicators
- Sparse-data indicators

### 5.2 Feature groups

Recommended feature groups:

- Historical value
- Recent engagement
- Engagement durability
- Product usage
- Subscription lifecycle
- Campaign response
- Content affinity
- Team/player affinity
- Program participation
- Partner context
- Market context
- Risk indicators
- Growth indicators
- Data quality / confidence indicators

### 5.3 Identity rules

The engine must define how fan identities are resolved and used.

Key questions:

- What is the primary fan identifier?
- How are anonymous, registered, subscribed, and purchasing identities connected?
- What identity confidence is required for activation?
- What identifiers are allowed in each downstream system?
- How are merges, splits, and identity updates handled?

### 5.4 Data eligibility

Every feature should have usage metadata.

Recommended fields:

- Allowed for modeling
- Allowed for activation
- Allowed for reporting
- Allowed for partner use

### 7.2 Example rule logic

Example 1: Save path eligibility

A fan is eligible for save-path treatment if:

- FEV tier is Enterprise Critical or High Value
- Retention risk is High
- Contact eligibility is true
- Confidence is Medium or High
- Fan is not already in conflicting treatment
- Holdout assignment allows treatment

Example 2: Conversion path eligibility

A fan is eligible for high-potential conversion treatment if:

- Growth potential is High
- Current direct revenue is Low or Zero
- Engagement durability is Medium or High
- Product fit exists
- Contact eligibility is true
- The fan is not over-contacted

Example 3: Paid media suppression

An audience may be suppressed from high-cost retargeting if:

- FEV is Low Current Priority
- Growth potential is Low
- Strategic value is Low
- Confidence is High
- No active strategic exception applies

Example 4: Product friction escalation

A friction issue should be escalated if:

- Affected fans include high-FEV or high-growth-potential fans
- The friction repeats across a meaningful audience
- The estimated value at risk crosses an agreed threshold
- Product owner is assigned

## 13. Technical operating runbook

### 13.1 Score refresh failure

If daily FEV scores fail to refresh:

1. Data Engineering investigates source or pipeline failure.
2. Platform owner flags stale score status.
3. Activation teams are notified if downstream treatment is affected.
4. Use prior-day scores only if approved fallback rules allow it.
5. Incident is documented with root cause and remediation.

### 13.2 Model version change

Before a new model version is promoted:

1. Data Science validates calibration and drift.
2. Analytics compares score movement and affected audience sizes.
3. Business owners review expected impact on decision plays.
4. Governance reviews material use-case changes if needed.
5. Rollback plan is documented.
6. Version is released with effective date and release notes.

### 13.3 Threshold change

Before thresholds are changed:

1. Business owner proposes decision rationale.
2. Analytics estimates audience impact.
3. Finance reviews economics where cost is material.
4. Experimentation confirms measurement plan.
5. Rule version is updated.
6. Change is documented in decision log.

### 13.4 Activation mismatch

If downstream systems receive incorrect or unexpected audiences:

1. Activation owner pauses affected journey or campaign if needed.
2. Martech/CDP owner checks delivery logs.
3. Data Engineering validates source output.
4. Rule owner checks eligibility logic.
5. Analytics checks treatment and holdout integrity.
6. Issue is documented before restart.

### 13.5 Data governance issue

If a data-use concern is raised:

1. Pause affected activation if risk is material.
2. Governance and Privacy review use case and fields.
3. Confirm consent and approved usage.
4. Remove or modify fields if needed.
5. Document decision and update allowed-use registry.

## 15. Technical playbook takeaway

The technical engine should not be judged by how sophisticated it looks. It should be judged by whether it makes the strategic playbook executable.

The engine must reliably answer:

- Who or what crossed a value condition?
- What action is eligible?
- Who owns the action?
- Can the action be activated safely?
- Was the action measured?
- Did it create incremental value?

If the engine cannot answer those questions, it is not yet a decision platform.

# Shared appendix: Executive summary version

Fan Enterprise Value should become the NBA's operating system for fan investment decisions.

The strategic playbook defines what the business should do when value, risk, potential, or strategic opportunity changes.

The technical playbook defines the engine that produces, governs, serves, activates, and measures those decisions.

The goal is not to create more scores. The goal is to make better decisions:

- Protect high-value fans at risk.
- Grow high-potential fans intelligently.
- Avoid wasting spend where value is unlikely.
- Invest patiently where strategic value is high.
- Fix friction where valuable fans are dropping off.
- Require incrementality before scaling campaigns or partner claims.

The operating standard is simple:

**No signal without an owner. No owner without an action. No action without measurement. No technical build without a decision use case.**
